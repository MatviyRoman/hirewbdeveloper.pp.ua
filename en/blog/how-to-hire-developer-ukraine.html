How to Hire a Web Developer
from Ukraine in 2025

March 2025Roman Matviy — Ukrainian developer working with EU/US clients since 201810 min read

Ukraine has one of the strongest tech talent pools in Eastern Europe. Despite the ongoing war, Ukrainian developers continue delivering high-quality remote work for clients worldwide. This guide covers everything a foreign client needs to know to hire successfully.

Why Hire a Ukrainian Developer?

Ukraine has one of the strongest tech talent pools in Eastern Europe. A few practical reasons international companies have been hiring Ukrainian developers since the mid-2000s:

  • Quality-to-cost ratio — Senior Ukrainian developers with 5–8 years of experience typically charge $25–$65/hr, versus $80–$150/hr for comparable skill in the US or UK.
  • Technical education — Strong STEM legacy from the Soviet era. Computer science and mathematics programs are rigorous.
  • English proficiency — Most professional developers communicate at B2–C1 level. Written English is generally very good.
  • European time zone — UTC+2 (UTC+3 in summer). Near-full overlap with Western Europe; 3–4 hour morning overlap with US East Coast.
  • Work ethic — Direct, solution-oriented, deadline-conscious. Working with international clients is a competitive differentiator, so developers are motivated to deliver.
War context (2025): Ukraine has been under full-scale Russian invasion since February 2022. Most developers work from Western Ukraine (Lviv, Uzhhorod, Chernivtsi) or abroad. Power stability and business continuity have largely been restored for remote tech workers.

Where to Find Ukrainian Developers

  • Upwork — Search for "Laravel developer Ukraine". Look for JSS above 90% and verified earnings.
  • Djinni.co — Ukraine's most popular tech job board. Browse developer profiles or post a job.
  • Toptal — Premium vetting. Higher cost but quality floor is high. Good for critical projects.
  • LinkedIn — Good for mid-senior roles. Filter by location and skills.
  • GitHub — Browse active contributors to open-source projects in your tech stack.
  • Direct referral — Ask developers or agencies you trust for referrals. Often the best channel.

How to Vet a Candidate

  • 1
    GitHub profile — Active contributions and public projects with readable code. An empty GitHub is a yellow flag (though many professionals work mostly on private repos).
  • 2
    Portfolio with live URLs — Ask what their specific contribution was on each project. Can they describe the architecture decisions they made?
  • 3
    30-minute technical call — Ask them to explain how they would architect a specific feature, what tools they would use, and why. Not a whiteboard algorithm test.
  • 4
    Small paid test ($200–$500) — Before committing to a larger engagement, offer a small paid task. This reveals communication style, code quality, and deadline reliability better than any interview.
  • 5
    References — Ask for 1–2 previous clients you can contact on LinkedIn. A professional developer will have these.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Rates that seem extremely low — a "senior" at $8/hr is almost certainly misrepresenting experience or will disappear after payment.
  • Vague or generic answers about your project — no questions asked, immediate quote sent.
  • Refuses a small paid test task.
  • Response time longer than 24 hours becomes a pattern before work even starts.
  • Cannot explain their past code decisions or architecture choices clearly.

Payment Methods

  • Wise (TransferWise) — Best for recurring payments. Low fees, fast, supports UAH and EUR accounts.
  • USDT / USDC — Very popular with Ukrainian freelancers. Instant, low fees, exchange-rate stable.
  • PayPal — Simple for one-off payments. Available in Ukraine with some withdrawal limitations.
  • Upwork / Toptal escrow — Use platform escrow for first engagements. Protects both parties.
  • SWIFT bank transfer — Slower and has fees. Better for larger project payments.

For US West Coast clients I recommend async-first: daily written updates, decisions documented in Notion or Linear, and one weekly video call. It works well with the right expectations.

Recommended Communication Stack

  • Telegram — Primary async channel. Fast, reliable, works everywhere in Ukraine.
  • Slack — For clients with existing workspaces.
  • Google Meet / Zoom — Weekly or bi-weekly video sync.
  • GitHub — Code, pull requests, code review.
  • Notion / Linear — Task tracking and documentation.
  • Loom — Async screen recordings for demos and walkthroughs.

Contract Basics

For short engagements, a simple email agreement with scope, rate, and payment terms is sufficient. For longer projects consider a freelance services agreement covering: IP assignment to the client, NDA if needed, milestone-based payment schedule, and a clear scope definition to prevent scope creep disputes.

Summary: Is It Worth It?

Yes — with the right vetting. The value proposition is strong: senior-level technical skill at mid-level Western prices, European time zone, and developers who are highly motivated to build long-term relationships with international clients. Vet properly, communicate clearly, and set up an async-friendly workflow. Done right, it is one of the best hiring decisions a growing company can make.
